    <details class="work-item">
      <summary>Negative Emotion in Art</summary>
      <div class="abstract">
        I address the paradox of painful art: we tend to be attracted to and voluntarily pursue painful art, such as tragedy and horror, that is expected to evoke negative emotions, such as pity and fear, typically regarded as aversive. Most existing accounts resolve the paradox by denying that our overall emotional experience of painful art is negative. They claim instead that pleasure dominates our emotional experience of painful art. I argue, however, that these accounts are based on problematic hedonic assumptions. I argue instead that the negative emotions experienced in painful art are intrinsically valuable and thus not wholly aversive. Thus, negative emotions per se are partly what people willingly seek when engaging with painful art.
      </div>
    </details>

    <details class="work-item">
      <summary>Weakness in Love</summary>
      <div class="abstract">
        I explore the puzzle of how lovers treat the beloved’s weaknesses and flaws. I argue that in some cases, perceiving someone’s weakness and flaws actually makes us love them more. Most existing theories of love show that weaknesses and flaws do not undermine love, yet they rarely explain why such traits can play a positive role in loving relationships. I suggest that this phenomenon can be explained by a typical and distinctive attitude of love—namely, a desire or disposition to understand the beloved.
      </div>
    </details>

    <details class="work-item">
      <summary>Experiential Imagination and Emotion in Fiction</summary>
      <div class="abstract">
        How can an audience have emotional responses towards fictional characters, situations, and events, knowing that they do not exist? I contend that people can and do experience genuine emotions even without believing in the existence of the objects of those emotions, and such emotions can sometimes be even more genuine and deeper. This is because the capacity of triggering emotional response among different mental states is related to their phenomenological characteristics. Experiential imagination is phenomenologically richer than either propositional imagination or belief. Thus, we can have rich emotional responses by having rich experiential imagination, even when engaging with fiction.
      </div>
    </details>
